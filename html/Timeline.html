TimeLine

1995

ESPN SportsZone streams a live radio broadcast of a baseball game between the Seattle Mariners and the New York Yankees to thousands of its subscribers worldwide using cutting-edge technology developed by a Seattle-based startup company named Real Networks – the first livestreaming event. Macromedia releases Shockwave Player for Netscape Navigator, which becomes the primary format of streaming media for the late 1990s and 2000s (until it is gradually supplanted by HTML5).

1998

MPEG-4, a method of defining compression of audio and visual (AV) digital data, is introduced.

1999

Microsoft introduces streaming feature in Windows Media Player 6.4. It introduces the ASF file format, which allows storage of multiple video and audio tracks inside a single file. It also introduces Windows Media streaming protocols that support switching streams during broadcast. This technology is most commonly referred to as Multiple Bit Rate ASF, or simply MBR. Apple introduces a streaming media format in its QuickTime 4 application.

2002

Adaptive bit rate over HTTP is created by the DVD Forum at the WG1 Special Streaming group.

2003

Adaptive bit rate over HTTP is created by the DVD Forum at the WG1 Special Streaming group.

2004

MindGeek is founded as Too Much Media. Its name is changed to Manwin in 2010, and then MindGeek in October 2013. Its operations are primarily related to Internet pornography, but also include other online properties such as the comedy video website videobash.com and celebrity gossip site celebs.com

2005

Google Video launches. Dailymotion, a French video-sharing website, is founded. YouTube opens for video uploads, and the first YouTube video uploaded on April 23, 2005 is titled Me at the zoo. Between March and July 2006, YouTube grows from 30 to 100 million views of videos per day.

2006

Crunchyroll, an American website and international online community focused on video streaming East Asian media including anime, manga, drama, music, electronic entertainment, and auto racing content, is founded. Justin.tv, a live-streaming service that is the parent company of Twitch, is founded by Justin Kan. Amazon introduces video on demand service Amazon Video.Google acquires YouTube. LiveLeak, a UK-based video sharing website that lets users post and share videos (often of reality footage, politics, war, and other world events), is founded.[20]Youku, one of China's top online video and streaming service platforms, is founded.

2007

Netflix announces that it will launch streaming video. HTML5 specification introduces the video element for the purpose of playing videos. This allows embedding video to no longer necessitate a third-party plugin, as it can be played natively in the browser. HTML5 would later overtake Flash as the primary mechanism for broadcasting video. Vevo is founded. It offers music videos from two of the "big three" major record labels, Universal Music Group and Sony Music Entertainment.Microsoft introduces Microsoft Silverlight, an application framework for writing and running rich Internet applications, similar to Adobe Flash.

2008

DivX announces that it will shut down Stage6,[27] stating that it is unable to continue to provide the attention and resources required for its continued operation. Macromedia Flash moves to the H.264 encoding codec. Hulu, an online streaming service for TV/movies, launches for public access in the United States.

2009

Google discontinues the ability to upload videos to Google Video

2010

iQiyi, an online video platform based in Beijing, China launches. Viki, an international video website offering TV shows, movies, and other premium content, is founded and gets Series A round funding.

2011

Dynamic Adaptive Streaming over HTTP – which enables high quality streaming of media content over the Internet delivered from conventional HTTP web servers – becomes a draft international standard.[35] The MPEG-DASH standard i s published as ISO/IEC 23009-1:2012 in April, 2012.Vudu announces the launch of its online streaming service. Manwin/MindGeek acquires the pornographic video sharing website YouPorn.[37]Justin.tv spins off its gaming division as Twitch.tv, which officially launches in public beta. Keek – a free online social networking service that allows its users to upload video status updates, which are called "keeks" – launches.

2012

Megaupload (and Megavideo) are shut down by the feds.[40]Vine, a short-form video sharing service where users can share six-second-long looping video clips, is founded by Dom Hofmann, Rus Yusupov, and Colin Kroll. Snapchat adds the ability to send video snaps in addition to photos.

2013

Instagram launches video sharing. YouTube drops Flash for HTML5 video as default.

2015

Periscope, a live video streaming app for iOS and Android developed by Kayvon Beykpour and Joe Bernstein is launched (and acquired by Twitter before its launch).
